01
The problem

A drug does not stay where you put it.

The moment a drug enters the blood, it starts a two-way trip. It spreads out into the body's tissues and, at the same time, the body begins clearing it away. Working out how its level changes over time is what separates a dose that helps from one that harms.

01 · CENTRAL

The blood

This is where the drug starts, in the blood and the organs it reaches quickly, like the heart, liver, and kidneys. The level here shoots up fast, then drops as the drug spreads out and gets cleared.

02 · PERIPHERAL

The tissues

Muscle, fat, and skin take up the drug more slowly. The level here drops away gently as the drug drifts back into the blood and slowly leaves the body.

03 · THE MATH

Two linked equations

Two simple equations describe how the drug moves between the blood and the tissues. We solve them exactly using eigenvalues, then test three other methods against that exact answer.

The journey of a drug
through the body.

Every medicine goes through four stages once you take it, known together as ADME: Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, and Excretion. The video below follows a dose through a see-through body and shows how each stage links to the two-compartment model we built for this project. It plays on its own, but you can pause it or jump to any stage.

AAbsorption
DDistribution
MMetabolism
EExcretion

How this links to our maths: our two-compartment model splits the body into the blood (the central compartment) and the tissues (the peripheral compartment). Absorption is the drug entering the blood. Distribution is the drug moving back and forth between the blood and the tissues, which is exactly what the transfer rates k12 and k21 describe in our equations. Metabolism and excretion are the liver and kidneys clearing the drug for good, which is what the elimination rate k10 stands for. So this whole video is really a picture of the same system our equations solve.

Live model

Move the rate constants.
Watch the body respond.

This tool solves the very same equations from the paper while you watch, using the Runge–Kutta method. Drag any slider and the two curves update straight away, so you can see how the different speeds change the way the drug moves.

Try this: turning up k₁₀ pulls both curves down faster, because the body clears the drug sooner. Turning up k₁₂ pushes the drug into the tissues harder, which makes the early peak in the blood sharper.

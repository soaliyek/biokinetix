Introduction

The real-world problem, why it matters, and how differential equations let researchers predict the journey of a drug through the human body.

Background

The science of where a drug goes

In clinical pharmacology and personalised medicine, understanding how a drug moves through the human body is fundamental to patient safety and therapeutic effectiveness. When a drug is administered intravenously, it does not remain uniformly distributed. Instead, it migrates between physiologically distinct regions at different rates. This dynamic process, if poorly understood, can result in under-dosing, toxicity, or outright treatment failure.

Pharmacokinetics is the scientific discipline that quantifies this behaviour. It describes how a drug is absorbed, distributed, metabolised, and excreted, collectively referred to as ADMET (Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion, and Toxicity). Among the mathematical frameworks used in pharmacokinetic analysis, compartmental modelling has proven to be one of the most powerful and widely applied approaches.

The two-compartment idea

The model divides the body into two interconnected regions: a central compartment (the bloodstream and highly perfused organs like the heart, the liver, and the kidneys), where the drug is introduced and distributes rapidly; and a peripheral compartment (tissues that receive the drug more slowly like the muscle, fat, skin). Drug transfer between them, and elimination from the body, follows first-order kinetics, expressible as a system of ordinary differential equations.

Why it matters

Mathematics in service of medicine

The accurate prediction of drug concentration over time is not a theoretical exercise, it has direct consequences for clinical decision-making:

Dosing intervals

If a drug clears the central compartment too quickly, it may fall below its therapeutic threshold before the next dose, rendering treatment ineffective.

Toxicity prevention

If distribution to the peripheral compartment is slower than expected, blood concentrations can climb to toxic levels.

Personalised medicine

Patients differ in weight, organ function, and age; all affecting rate constants. Accurate modelling enables dose individualisation.

Previous studies have applied the two-compartment model to a wide range of drugs and delivery routes: from intravenous levodopa for Parkinson's disease, to the antibiotic sisomicin in healthy volunteers, to the antihistamine mizolastine. In each case, the model characterised drug distribution and elimination with clinical precision.

Objectives

What the paper set out to do

The study by Fatima et al. (2025), published in Mathematical and Computational Applications, presents a rigorous investigation of intravascular drug concentration dynamics using the two-compartment open pharmacokinetic model. The objectives addressed in the paper are:

  1. To formulate and explain the system of ODEs governing drug concentration in both compartments.
  2. To derive the analytical (exact) solution using eigenvalue decomposition and matrix methods.
  3. To implement and compare three numerical methods: the Euler method, the fourth-order Runge–Kutta method, and the Adams–Bashforth–Moulton method.
  4. To conduct a thorough error analysis, quantifying each method's deviation from the exact solution.
  5. To investigate model parameters through sensitivity analysis.
  6. To examine the modifications the original researchers introduced to better reflect real-world behaviour.

How differential equations are used

The rate of change of drug concentration in each compartment depends on the current concentrations in both. That coupling, rate of change expressed in terms of present state, is exactly what a differential equation captures. Solving the system reveals the full concentration-time profile from a single injected dose.

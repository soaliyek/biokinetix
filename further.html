Further Research

Where this model could go next, and the bigger questions that build on what we have done so far.

Future directions

Five ways to take this further

01

Models with memory

This model assumes the rate of change depends only on what is happening right now. But the body sometimes carries a kind of memory, where past states still have an effect. A different type of maths, called fractional calculus, can capture this and may work better for drugs that stick to the tissues in complicated ways.

02

When clearing slows down

The model assumes the body always clears the drug at a steady pace. At high doses, though, the body's enzymes can get overwhelmed and the clearing slows. Adding a rule for this, known as Michaelis–Menten kinetics, would let the model handle painkillers, HIV drugs, and others that behave this way.

03

Adding more compartments

Some drugs reach deep places like bone marrow or the brain, which may need a third compartment to describe properly. More detailed models can even give each major organ its own compartment with its own measured values. They paint a fuller picture, but they are also harder to build and solve.

04

Differences between patients

The speed values change with age, weight, kidney health, and genes. Instead of one fixed number, researchers can treat each value as a range that covers a whole population. This is what makes it possible to fine-tune a dose for each individual.

05

Smarter step sizes

The Adams–Bashforth–Moulton method can be thrown off by a shaky start. Some methods change their step size on the fly, taking small careful steps when things move fast and bigger ones when things are calm, which saves time without losing accuracy.

06

Our own change, coming next

For the next submission, our team will make our own change to the model and use it to look at a similar real-life situation that we will design ourselves.

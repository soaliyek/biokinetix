Section 02

The Model

Every part of the body, every symbol, and every equation, explained step by step and then solved.

Overview

A body in two compartments

The two-compartment model describes what happens after a single dose is injected straight into the bloodstream all at once, at the start time we call $t = 0$. We treat the body as two connected spaces, and the drug flows between them at a steady pace. The rule is simple: the more drug there is in one space, the faster it flows out of that space into the next.

Figure: The two-compartment open model. Drug enters the central compartment, exchanges with the peripheral compartment via $k_{12}$ and $k_{21}$, and is eliminated via $k_{10}$.

Governing equations

From amounts to concentrations

The amount of drug in each compartment is linked to its concentration by the size, or volume, of that compartment:

$$A_c(t) = C_c(t)\cdot V_1$$(1)
$$A_p(t) = C_p(t)\cdot V_2$$(2)

Doctors measure concentration in the blood, not the raw amount, so we write the equations in terms of concentration. This gives us the two equations at the heart of the model:

$$\frac{dC_c}{dt} = -(k_{12} + k_{10})\,C_c + k_{21}\,C_p$$(3)
$$\frac{dC_p}{dt} = k_{12}\,C_c - k_{21}\,C_p$$(4)

Reading the first equation

This one tracks the blood (the central compartment). Three things change its level at the same time:

  • $-k_{12}\,C_c$ is drug leaving the blood and moving into the tissues.
  • $-k_{10}\,C_c$ is drug leaving the body for good, broken down or passed out.
  • $+k_{21}\,C_p$ is drug coming back from the tissues, which pushes the blood level up again.

Reading the second equation

This one tracks the tissues (the peripheral compartment). Only two things matter here:

  • $+k_{12}\,C_c$ is drug arriving from the blood.
  • $-k_{21}\,C_p$ is drug heading back to the blood.
Linear algebra

The matrix form & exact solution

Written compactly, the system is $\dfrac{d\mathbf{C}}{dt} = \mathbf{A}\,\mathbf{C}(t)$, where:

$$\mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} -(k_{12}+k_{10}) & k_{21} \\ k_{12} & -k_{21} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -0.55 & \phantom{-}0.25 \\ \phantom{-}0.50 & -0.25 \end{bmatrix}$$

The eigenvalues of $\mathbf{A}$ solve the characteristic equation $\det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I}) = 0$:

$$\lambda^2 + 0.8\lambda + 0.0125 = 0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \lambda_1 = -0.0159,\;\; \lambda_2 = -0.7841$$

Both eigenvalues come out negative. That is a good sign, because it means the drug level always falls toward zero over time, which is exactly what should happen as the body clears the drug. Putting in the starting values $C_c(0) = 0$ and $C_p(0) = 200$ gives the exact answer for each compartment. These two equations are the benchmark we test the other methods against:

$$C_c(t) = 65.089\,e^{-0.0159\,t} - 65.09\,e^{-0.7841\,t}$$
$$C_p(t) = 139.05\,e^{-0.0159\,t} + 60.94\,e^{-0.7841\,t}$$

Why the exact answer matters

Because these equations can be solved exactly, we have a perfect yardstick. Every other method we try can be checked against it to see how close it gets. That turns the whole project from guesswork into a fair, measurable test.

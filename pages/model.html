Mathematical Model

Two-Compartment Pharmacokinetic Model

A mathematical description of how an intravenously administered drug distributes between the bloodstream and body tissues, and how it is eliminated over time — governed by a coupled system of first-order differential equations.

01 Model Structure

The body is divided into two connected compartments. The central compartment represents blood plasma and highly perfused organs; the peripheral compartment represents slowly perfused tissues. A single intravenous (IV) bolus dose enters the central compartment, from which the drug distributes to the periphery and is eliminated from the body.

04 Governing Equations

Amount–concentration relationship

Equations 1–2
A_c(t) = C_c(t)\cdot V_1 \qquad\qquad A_p(t) = C_p(t)\cdot V_2

Core system of ODEs (concentration form)

Dividing the amount equations by their volumes gives the primary governing equations solved throughout the model:

Equation 5 · Central compartment
\frac{dC_c}{dt} = -\left(k_{12} + k_{10}\right) C_c + k_{21}\, C_p
Equation 6 · Peripheral compartment
\frac{dC_p}{dt} = k_{12}\, C_c - k_{21}\, C_p

What each term represents

TermPharmacokinetic meaning
−k₁₂·Cccentral Drug leaving for the peripheral compartment (distribution out)
−k₁₀·Cccentral Drug eliminated from the body (metabolism / excretion)
+k₂₁·Cpcentral Drug returning from the peripheral compartment
+k₁₂·Ccperipheral Drug arriving from the central compartment
−k₂₁·Cpperipheral Drug returning to the central compartment

Matrix (state-space) form

Compact form
\mathbf{C}'(t) = \mathbf{A}\,\mathbf{C}(t), \quad \mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} -(k_{12}+k_{10}) & k_{21} \\ k_{12} & -k_{21} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -0.55 & 0.25 \\ 0.50 & -0.25 \end{bmatrix}

05 Analytical (Exact) Solution

Solving the eigenvalue problem of A yields two eigenvalues that correspond to the fast distributive phase and the slow terminal phase:

Eigenvalues
\lambda^2 + 0.8\lambda + 0.0125 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \lambda_1 = -0.0159,\quad \lambda_2 = -0.7841

Applying the initial conditions gives the closed-form bi-exponential solutions for each compartment:

Equation 29 · Central
C_c(t) = 65.089\,e^{-0.0159\,t} - 65.09\,e^{-0.7841\,t}
Equation 30 · Peripheral
C_p(t) = 139.05\,e^{-0.0159\,t} - 60.94\,e^{-0.7841\,t}
The first exponential term (slow λ₁) captures the terminal elimination phase; the second (fast λ₂) captures the rapid initial distribution.

06 Numerical Methods & Accuracy

The ODE system is solved at discrete steps (Δt = 0.1 h) using three methods, then compared against the exact solution. Lower overall error is better.

MethodCentral errorPeripheral errorOverall error
Euler (1st order)0.27710.295328.62%
Adams–Bashforth–Moulton0.06630.08687.66%
4th-order Runge–Kutta  ✓0.00860.02211.54%

The fourth-order Runge–Kutta method tracks the analytical solution most closely, with an overall average error of just 1.54% — making it the recommended scheme for this system.
